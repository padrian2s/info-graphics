Strategic middle layer for AI bets

AI is not “transforming everything” equally — it’s splitting the economy.

Most AI talk is either too abstract (“AI changes everything”) or too tactical (“use ChatGPT for customer support”). What leaders need is a map of how competition changes — where your business becomes vulnerable, and where it becomes (ironically) protected.

The missing lens

1-minute read

AI collapses the marginal cost of cognitive tasks that can be expressed in language (drafting, summarizing, researching, coding, generating variants).

When production becomes abundant, the bottleneck moves to judgment + accountability — and to physical execution where atoms must still move in the real world.

The barbell economy (bifurcation)

competition map
Digital, contestable markets
Output easy to compare, switching easy → baseline commoditized, competition intensifies.
Middle tier gets crushed
Professional services + mid-market shops selling cognitive production → squeezed from below & above.
Physical, local markets
Relationship + location constraints → AI lowers overhead but doesn’t raise contestability → margins can rise.
Contestabilitythe key concept

A market is contestable when buyers can easily evaluate alternatives and switch. Switching to a marketing agency across the world is easy. Switching to a plumber across the world is… not.

Distributionthe scarce resource

As production commoditizes, distribution, bundling, embedded workflows, and trust become more valuable. Giants with distribution moats can benefit disproportionately.

The 3-layer map of value

diagnostic lens
1

Tokenizable cognition (now abundant)

Drafting, summarizing, researching, coding, generating variants. AI collapses marginal cost.

2

Judgment + accountability (bottleneck)

Choosing the right option, owning outcomes, taste, ethics, liability, relationships, trust.

3

Physical execution (atoms)

Installation, repair, care, delivery — constrained by time, place, and the physical world.

Rule of thumb: If you mostly sell Layer 1 output, you’re exposed. If you mostly sell Layer 2 judgment, you’re resilient. If you mostly sell Layer 3 execution, you’re structurally advantaged (AI helps overhead but can’t “teleport” the service).

1) Cost collapse → output explosion (Jevons-style effect)

When something gets cheaper, we often use more of it, not less. Cheaper “cognition” means: more drafts, more experiments, more segments, more analysis. The bottleneck shifts to judgment.

2) Baumol-style effect: what can’t be automated gets relatively pricier

Death trap to avoid: using AI only to make a legacy Layer‑1 production model ~20% more efficient. If you’re competing with 3-person AI-native teams, that’s often just “dying slower.”

Investment playbook (by position)

actionable bets
Mid-tier in contestable digital marketshighest risk

Path A: Get radically lean (overhead + headcount + process) to compete with tiny AI-native teams.
Path B: Move up the stack: stop selling deliverables; sell judgment, accountability, quality, outcomes.
AI spend: tools that amplify senior review, client trust, QA, compliance, and workflow integration.

Physical, local, relationship-heavy servicestailwind

AI lowers administrative burden without making the market meaningfully more contestable.
AI spend: scheduling, dispatch, quoting, customer comms, invoicing/collections, internal ops.
Beware “transformation theater” — you often want boring reliability + efficiency.

AI-native startups selling “production”commodity risk

Pure cognitive production is a depreciating asset — models get cheaper, margins compress.
Run toward moats: distribution, embedded workflows, switching costs, compliance/audit, human-in-the-loop review, liability/accountability wrappers, orchestration.

Giants with distribution moatsprotected

AI can make your moat more valuable: trust + bundling + platform status stand out as production commoditizes.
Main risk: internal speed + talent flight (startups hire your best people).
AI spend: internal enablement, rapid tooling, training, governance that accelerates adoption (not blocks it).

What “good” looks likenorth star

Use AI to flood Layer 1 with options — then build the systems, incentives, and workflows that make Layer 2 decisions faster, safer, and more accountable. If you touch the real world, use AI to remove back-office friction so you can do more of the physical work that stays scarce.

Decision checklistask this weekly

1) Are we selling scarce judgment or abundant production?
2) Are customers able to compare/switch easily?
3) Do we have distribution or embedded workflow advantage?
4) Is our AI spend reducing overhead or reinforcing a doomed model?
5) What bottleneck becomes binding as production gets cheaper?
